Part 1 · Orientation

What this report is

The pre-market report is a decision-support briefing, generated before the open each trading day, covering your AI-infrastructure book. It does not tell you what to trade. It assembles, in one place, the overnight facts and the institutional-positioning signals you'd otherwise hunt across a dozen sources — so you can form a judgment fast.

The core philosophy — two systems, one report

Everything in the report serves a deliberate split that runs through your whole process:

▸ THE ENGINE (systematic)

A rules-based momentum + earnings-beat model picks and sizes the book monthly. It is excellent at the systematic baseline and structurally blind to forward competitive shifts and external shocks.

▸ THE OPERATOR (you)

You apply forward judgment where the engine is blind — the INTC/NVDA override is the live example. The report's job is to feed your overlay with what the engine can't see.

Read the report as the operator's instrument panel: the data sections are the gauges; you are the pilot.

When it's generated
Pre-market (typically 6–8 AM ET). Prices reflect yesterday's close; futures and news describe this morning's setup. The header date and timestamp tell you exactly when it was built — always confirm it matches today before acting.
Part 2 · Workflow

The 5-minute morning workflow

Read top-to-bottom, but with intent. This is the order that gets you to a decision fastest:

1
Assessment + What Changed Overnight
The 30-second orientation. The amber assessment banner gives the posture (CAUTIOUS / HOLD etc.); the bullets are the overnight facts. If nothing here surprises you, the day is "manage, don't react."
2
Conviction Dashboard strip
One glance: is Theme-RS still GREEN? Any VOL ALERTS? Days to rebalance? This confirms the system's state before you look at any single name.
3
Overnight SEC filings (8-K) + Earnings calendar
Did any holding file a material event overnight? Is anything reporting today or tomorrow (→ E5 blackout)? These are the items that force action or restraint.
4
Positions table — relative strength
Scan the VS-SEC and RS-vs-SPY columns. Who's leading, who's breaking down relative to peers? This is where a deteriorating name shows up before the headline does.
5
Positioning signals (GEX, dark pool, flow, SI)
The "why" behind the moves. Use these to confirm or question what the price is doing — e.g., is INTC's slide backed by dealer negative-gamma and distribution, or is it noise?
6
Bottom Line
The synthesis + action items. If you only have 60 seconds, read the Assessment and the Bottom Line; everything between is the evidence for them.
The one rule
The report is built to be read, not obeyed. Every "action item" is a prompt for your judgment, anchored to the engine + overlay split. Disagreeing with it is allowed and expected.

§5 Options Positioning & GEX

This is the densest and most valuable section — it shows where options dealers are positioned, which shapes how a stock (or SPY) tends to move. Four pieces:

Market Tide

Market-wide net call vs put premium. A read on whether the whole tape's options flow is leaning bullish or bearish. Context, not a trade trigger.

The SPY GEX number line

The single most useful positioning visual. It plots three levels around the current SPY price:

SPY · GEX NUMBER LINE ($698 — $759)
Put Wall $698
Call Wall $759
SPY $758.54
1
Put Wall (red) — the strike with the most negative gamma below spot. Tends to act as a support shelf; a decisive break below can accelerate selling.
2
Call Wall (green) — the heaviest positive-gamma strike above spot. Tends to act as a resistance ceiling / magnet into expiry.
3
Spot (cyan dot) — where SPY actually is. When spot sits right at the call wall (as drawn here), the index is "pinned" — dealers have little room to let it run higher without a fresh catalyst. This is exactly the "records, then a pause" setup.
Why the labels stagger
When spot and a wall are very close (under ~1% apart), the labels drop to different rows so they stay readable. Staggered labels = the levels are clustered, which is itself a signal (a pinned tape).

Per-position GEX & the gamma flip

For your top holdings, the report shows each name's call wall, put wall, and gamma flip — the price below which dealers flip to negative gamma. The rule:

▸ SPOT ABOVE FLIP

Dealers in positive gamma — they dampen moves (sell rallies, buy dips). Volatility suppressed. Calmer tape.

▸ SPOT BELOW FLIP

Dealers in negative gamma — they amplify moves (sell weakness, buy strength). Downside accelerates. This is INTC right now ($107.93 vs $114.5 flip).

Live example — why this matters
INTC trading below its gamma flip means its slide isn't just fundamental (the NVDA threat) — dealer hedging is mechanically amplifying the downside. A small technical confirmation layered on the forward thesis. It does not change the hold decision, but it explains the velocity.

Max Pain

The strike where the most options expire worthless — options theory says price is gently "pulled" toward it into Friday expiry. The table shows each name vs its max pain. ABOVE MP = running ahead of options positioning (mild downward pull). BELOW MP = upward pull into expiry. A weak, secondary signal — useful as a tiebreaker, not a thesis.

IV Rank

Where each name's implied volatility sits in its 1-year range. CHEAP (<25) options are inexpensive to buy; EXPENSIVE (>75) options are pricey — typical after an earnings move. Tells you whether buying options here is rich or cheap; doesn't predict direction.

Section Guide

DEX / Vanna / Charm

The institutional-grade extension of GEX. Gamma is the headline Greek; these three add dimension to how dealer hedging will shift:

NET DEX
Delta exposure. The directional hedging pressure dealers carry right now. Large positive DEX → dealers are net long deltas and will sell into strength to stay hedged. The directional cousin of GEX.
NET VANNA
Sensitivity to IV. How dealer hedging shifts as implied volatility moves. High vanna means a change in fear/calm (VIX up or down) will force mechanical hedging flows — relevant around events and vol spikes.
NET CHARM
Decay into expiry. How hedging drifts simply as time passes toward expiration. Charm flows build into Friday/OPEX and can create predictable end-of-week drift.
How to use them
Treat DEX/Vanna/Charm as second-order confirmation, not primary triggers. Big vanna into a jobs report or Fed day = expect vol-driven hedging flows. Big charm into OPEX = expect drift toward walls. They explain the character of moves, layered on top of the gamma picture.

§6 Institutional Flow · §8 Dark Pool

Unusual Whales Flow Alerts

Notable options trades — large, unusual, or sweep orders — ranked by premium. These are footprints of size positioning. Useful for spotting where big money is placing directional bets, especially on your names. High volume-to-OI ratio = fresh positioning, not closing.

Dark Pool — Institutional Footprints

Off-exchange block trades, which is where institutions move size quietly. Two tables: a per-name aggregate (total premium, volume, biggest print) and the notable block prints (individual large trades). The key column is direction:

AT ASK / ABOVE MID ↑ = aggressive buying (accumulation) AT BID / BELOW MID ↓ = aggressive selling (distribution)
How direction is derived
Each block's print price is compared to the prevailing bid/ask (NBBO). A print at/above the ask means a buyer paid up → accumulation. At/below the bid → a seller hit the bid → distribution. Scan your memory complex (MU/SNDK/WDC) and INTC here: accumulation under a falling price is a divergence worth noting.

Section Guide

Short Interest & Squeeze Monitor

FINRA short-interest data (settled bi-monthly) plus daily short volume. The squeeze read is driven by Days-to-Cover (DTC) — how many days of average volume it would take shorts to cover.

DTC <2 = MINIMAL DTC 2–3 = LOW DTC 3–5 = MODERATE DTC >5 = ELEVATED squeeze fuel

The Daily SV% column shows what fraction of today's volume was short selling — a real-time sentiment shift on top of the bi-monthly snapshot. NET SHORT = sellers pressing; NET LONG = buyers in control.

Coverage caveat — important
NYSE-listed names (CIEN, DELL) don't appear in Nasdaq's short-interest feed and rely on a Yahoo Finance fallback. If they ever show UNAVAILABLE, the fallback library isn't loaded — it's a data-plumbing gap, not zero short interest. (Nasdaq-listed names like INTC/MU/AMD always populate directly.)

Insiders · Congress · Overnight 8-Ks

Overnight SEC Filings (8-K)

The newest and arguably most valuable addition. It scans all holdings for 8-K material-event filings in the last ~72h, reads the SEC item code, and tells you what happened — automatically. This is fact, not commentary.

Items 1.05 / 4.02 / 5.01 = high materiality 1.01 / 2.01 / 5.02 = notable 5.07 / 7.01 / 9.01 = routine

Example from a recent report: TXN · Item 5.02 Officer/Director Change — flagged automatically the morning it filed. If this section says "no material filings," that's a verified all-clear, not a gap.

Congressional Trading & Form 4 Insiders

Two "smart-money" layers. Congressional = Senate/House disclosures on your names (STOCK Act). Form 4 = corporate insiders buying/selling. Watch the [10b5-1] flag on insider sells — that marks a pre-scheduled sale (automatic, low signal) versus a discretionary one (more meaningful).

How much weight to give these
Supporting color, not primary signals. A cluster of insider buys is more meaningful than scattered scheduled sells. Congressional trades lag (disclosed weeks after the trade) — treat as sentiment, not timing.

§9 Earnings · ★ Bottom Line

The Earnings Calendar flags any holding reporting soon and applies the E5 blackout rule — no trading a name inside its earnings window. The Bottom Line synthesizes the whole report into a posture + numbered action items.

E5 Blackout — a hard rule
When a name is flagged E5 BLACKOUT (earnings within ~14 days, currently CIEN before its 6/4 print), you do not trade it until after the report — even if it ran up into the print, even if a signal looks tempting. The blackout protects you from binary-event risk. It's the one place the report tells you don't act, and it means it.

Signal glossary

Theme-RS
Theme Relative Strength — the engine's regime gate ranking SMH (semis) vs the 11 sector ETFs. GREEN/YELLOW/RED sets your deployment level.
GEX
Gamma Exposure. Aggregate dealer gamma by strike. Positive-gamma zones suppress volatility; negative-gamma zones amplify it.
Gamma Flip
The price level where net dealer gamma crosses from positive to negative. Below it, moves get amplified.
Call / Put Wall
The strikes with the heaviest positive (call) / negative (put) gamma. Act as resistance / support magnets.
DEX
Delta Exposure — directional dealer hedging pressure.
Vanna
Hedging sensitivity to changes in implied volatility.
Charm
Hedging drift as time decays toward expiry.
Max Pain
Strike where most options expire worthless; a weak magnet into expiry.
IV Rank
Where implied vol sits in its 1-year range. High = options expensive.
DTC
Days-to-Cover. Short interest ÷ average daily volume. Higher = more squeeze fuel.
Dark Pool
Off-exchange venues where institutions trade size. Direction (vs NBBO) reveals accumulation vs distribution.
Market Tide
Market-wide net options premium — a broad bullish/bearish sentiment gauge.
10b5-1
A pre-scheduled insider trading plan. Sales under it are automatic → low signal.
E5 Blackout
No-trade window around a holding's earnings (~14 days prior). A hard rule.
Relative Strength (RS)
A name's return measured against SPY or its sector — strips out the market move to show true out/under-performance.
Part 4 · Playbooks

Decision playbooks

The earnings playbook

✓
Beat + raised guidance
HOLD. The thesis is working. Do not trim into strength.
≈
Beat + flat / cautious guidance
Trim 25–30% next open. The quarter was fine but forward signal softened.
✕
Miss
Full review. Re-underwrite the position before deciding.
The discipline
Never panic-trim a beat on a sell-the-news dip. The report's positioning data (is the dip dealer-driven? is it being accumulated in dark pools?) helps you tell a real problem from noise.

When does a signal mean ACT vs WATCH?

▸ ACT

A hard rule triggers (E5 blackout, Theme-RS flips to RED), the engine rebalances, or your forward thesis changes (the INTC override). Action comes from rules and judgment.

▸ WATCH

A single positioning signal looks interesting (negative gamma, a dark-pool block, high IV). These inform, they don't trigger. One signal is a data point, not a decision.

The INTC override — the model case

INTC is the live example of operator-over-engine. The engine ranks it #2 (huge momentum + a near-zero-base earnings "beat") and wants to add. You hold and don't add because the forward NVDA PC-chip threat — invisible to any backward-looking signal — is the thing that matters. The report's job here is to surface the evidence (the 5-day RS breakdown, the below-flip negative gamma, the distribution in dark pools) so your forward judgment is well-armed. The engine self-corrects slowly; you act on the future now.

Part 5 · Honesty

Data sources & caveats

What's authoritative, what's derived, and what to trust accordingly:

Authoritative
Prices (Polygon), short interest (Nasdaq/FINRA), 8-K events & Form 4 (SEC via Polygon), economic calendar (FRED), options data (Unusual Whales), congressional (FMP). These are pulled programmatically and are facts.
Hand-assembled
The geopolitical / AI narrative prose is written and verified against same-morning sources. Well-sourced color — but it's the section most prone to staleness if the report is read late. Always check the header timestamp.
Derived proxy
The Smart $ column is computed from dark-pool direction + options flow — a reasonable proxy, not a standalone proprietary score. The geo overlay is a data-driven flag, advisory only.
Known gaps
NYSE short interest (CIEN, DELL) needs the yfinance fallback loaded. Some options sub-sections show only top-5 names by value. SI settles bi-monthly — it's not real-time.
The honest summary
The report is strongest as a positioning and event aggregator — it tells you what institutions are doing and what filed overnight, accurately. It is weakest where it relies on hand-written market color. Lean on the data sections for evidence; treat the prose as a well-informed briefing, not gospel; and remember the whole thing exists to arm your judgment, never to replace it.
